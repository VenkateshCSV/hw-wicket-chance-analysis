More Balanced Matches?

02 — Key Findings

Narrative summary

Wickets falling but below target: Mean wickets dropped from ~1.8 to ~1.3 across ranks — the feature is mechanically increasing wicket probability, but the net effect on wickets fallen is actually a reduction. Players are facing fewer balls overall (innings collapse faster), so total wickets per innings is lower despite higher per-ball wicket chance.
First innings is taking the hit: In the first innings, wickets are increasing (e.g. Proven: +8.8%, Remarkable: +30%) and scores are coming down. This is the intended effect — lower targets being set.
Second innings shows defensive adaptation: The chasing team, knowing the target is low, is playing conservatively — shifting shots toward zeros and ones, avoiding risky sixes. Fewer wickets fall in the second innings as a result. The match becomes one-sided in a new direction: chaser wins by wickets instead of loser winning by runs.
First 2 overs show the mechanism working correctly: In the first 2 overs, mean scores are slightly higher in the test case — players haven't yet adapted. Wickets in overs 1–2 are directionally higher in the first innings. This confirms the feature fires as designed early in the innings.
Win rate skew is a sample artefact + real signal: First batting WR collapses to <2% in the test (vs ~18–26% base), second batting WR jumps to ~87–92%. Small sample amplifies this, but the direction is real — lower targets are being chased down much more easily.

02 — Shot Selection Insights

Twos are the biggest casualty: Across all views, Two% drops sharply in the test — especially at Masterful and Supreme+ (−17% to −22% delta in full match). Players are moving away from running twos, likely because those balls also carry higher wicket risk now.
Zeros and Ones rise in the 2nd innings full match: At Masterful/Supreme+, Zero% rises +15–20% in second batting. Players are explicitly blocking or defending to reach a low target without risking wickets — confirming the defensive adaptation hypothesis.
First 2 overs, first batting shows no meaningful shot selection change: Shot distribution is broadly flat in overs 1–2 of the first innings. Players are not consciously selecting safer shots early — wickets are rising here purely from the mechanical probability increase, not from changed behaviour.
Sixes drop in first batting, rise slightly in second: In the first innings, sixes come down (−7% to −13%), suggesting some awareness of the higher wicket risk for sixes. In the second innings, six% actually rises slightly — chasing teams hit the final runs quickly with a power shot once they're close to the target.
