02 — Key Findings

Narrative summary

Mean wickets up, median unchanged: Mean wickets rise ~9–13% across brackets, but even so it was not enough to shift the median wickets fallen — q50 stays at 2 in every bracket and view. The feature's core stated goal (median 1→3) is not achieved.
Players did not meaningfully change shot selection: The shot mix moves less than 1 percentage point in almost every bracket. The only consistent shift is a small dip in fours (−0.4 to −0.9pp) with a marginal rise in ones — minor risk-aversion, not a behavioural change.
Scores are flat: Mean scores move −0.6% to −2.2% overall. Because behaviour didn't change and the wicket bump was small, totals and targets are effectively unchanged.
First 2 overs show a larger relative wicket bump (+16–22%): The mechanical effect is most visible early, before any (minimal) adaptation. But on a small base — q50 wickets in the first 2 overs stays at 1.
Read vs earlier analysis: The earlier dramatic collapse (wickets −25 to −45%, scores down sharply) was an artefact of bot matches and sample skew. This corrected read shows the feature has a much smaller effect than first thought.

02 — Shot Selection Insights

Almost no behavioural change: Across every bracket and view, the shot mix shifts less than 1pp. Players faced a higher wicket risk but kept playing essentially the same shots.
Fours are the only consistent mover: Down −0.4 to −0.9pp, with a small offsetting rise in ones (+0.4 to +0.5pp). A slight pull-back from the riskiest scoring shot toward safe singles — but tiny.
First 2 overs are virtually identical: Players open the same way regardless of arm. The minor fours→ones drift only appears later in the innings, and mostly at higher brackets.
Implication: The wicket-chance increase was too small to trigger the intended squad-diversification behaviour. A larger increase would be needed to change how players actually play.
